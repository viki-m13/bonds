How the strategy works — in plain English

The big idea, in one paragraph

Instead of picking one “best” way to trade, PHOENIX runs five different trading strategies at the same time and blends them together. Each strategy tries to make money using a different kind of signal — one watches trends, another watches fear (VIX), another uses a computer model, and so on. Because they use different logic, they rarely make losing bets on the same day. When one is stuck, another is working. Blended together, the ups and downs cancel out, and the result is a much smoother, more reliable return than any single one on its own. On top of that, a simple “brake” reduces risk automatically when the market gets scary.

The five strategies (each runs like its own little trader)

1. VANGUARD — 23.6% of portfolio  ·  The momentum trader
Picks whatever ETFs have gone up the most in the past 3 months and rides the trend. But it watches a “risk thermometer” — two numbers (VIX, which measures stock-market fear, and junk-bond spreads, which show when companies are stressed). If the thermometer runs hot, VANGUARD goes to cash and waits. Rebalances roughly monthly.
2. ORION — 32.7% of portfolio  ·  The balanced trader
Combines four different signals (momentum, trend, low-volatility, and risk-on/risk-off). When stocks look healthy, it buys stock ETFs. When they don't, it switches to safe assets like long-dated bonds or gold. Rebalances every Friday.
3. HELIOS — 18.5% of portfolio  ·  The “parent ETF” trader
Leveraged ETFs (like TQQQ or UPRO) follow a parent index (like the NASDAQ-100 or the S&P 500). HELIOS studies the parent indexes, not the leveraged versions directly — this avoids some of the weird decay noise you get from leveraged funds. When a parent looks strong, it buys the matching leveraged version. Rebalances every Friday.
4. QUANTUM — 15.2% of portfolio  ·  The machine-learning trader
A machine-learning model (XGBoost) was trained once on 9 years of history (2010–2018) to rank which ETFs are most likely to go up over the next ~3 weeks. It looks at about 30 different data points — recent returns, volatility, interest rates, credit spreads, etc. Then every 3 weeks it picks its top 3 and holds them. The model was trained once and never touched again — so the 2019–2026 results are a fair test.
5. CRYPTO — 10.1% of portfolio  ·  The Bitcoin trader
When Bitcoin has been going up over the past 3 months AND the overall stock market looks healthy, it holds a Bitcoin ETF (IBIT). Otherwise it sits in cash. Bitcoin's returns are almost unrelated to the other four strategies, so even a small slice adds diversification value. Rebalances every Friday.

Why five and not just one?

Every single one of these strategies has years where it loses money. VANGUARD lost money in 2015, 2022, and 2023. CRYPTO lost money in 2018 and 2022. But they almost never lose on the same days (their daily returns have near-zero correlation). So when HELIOS is having a bad week, QUANTUM is often having a good one, and they partially cancel out. Combining five uncorrelated streams of returns gives a much smoother ride than any one alone — like taking five different routes to work instead of just one. If one route has traffic, the average of all five is still fine.

The three safety brakes

On top of the five strategies, there are three automatic safety mechanisms that can reduce risk:

  • Daily vol target (15% target). If the portfolio's recent daily swings are bigger than expected (i.e., it's getting volatile), the strategy shifts some money into cash (BIL, a T-bill ETF). If swings are small, it stays fully invested. It never uses borrowed money or leverage above 100% — only the leverage built into the LETFs themselves.
  • Drawdown throttle. If the portfolio has fallen more than about 10% from its recent peak, it automatically reduces exposure further and moves more to cash. This prevents small losses from becoming catastrophic ones.
  • Volatility regime gate. If the market enters an extreme-vol regime (top 1% of the past year), exposure is cut in half. Very rare event, but when it fires, it fires hard.

What “LETF” means and why we use them

A leveraged ETF (“LETF”) is an exchange-traded fund that magnifies the daily return of some index. For example, TQQQ tries to deliver 3× the daily return of the NASDAQ-100. If NASDAQ is up 1% today, TQQQ is up about 3% (and if NASDAQ is down 1%, TQQQ is down about 3%). This gives you more “bang for your buck” when you're right, but bigger losses when you're wrong — which is exactly why we need good timing signals and the safety brakes above. We use LETFs across seven asset classes: U.S. stocks (UPRO, TQQQ, SSO, QLD), sectors (SOXL, TECL, FAS), real estate (DRN), international (EDC, YINN), commodities (UCO, UGL, NUGT), and long-dated bonds (TMF, UBT, TYD). Plus a spot Bitcoin ETF (IBIT) and T-bills (BIL) for cash.

How often it trades

The strategy generates a signal every evening (after market close, Monday through Friday). On most days there are 0 to 3 small trades, because most of the individual strategies don't rebalance every day. Total annual turnover is about 15× (meaning you replace about 15% of your holdings on an average week). This is active trading, but not high-frequency — a person can easily execute it manually each morning with 2-3 market-on-open orders.

The honest risk picture

No strategy is guaranteed. PHOENIX can lose money. In the backtest (2010–2026), the worst peak-to-trough drop was about −17.6% — better than the S&P 500's historical −30 to −50% bear-market losses, but still meaningful. The strategy has been stress-tested against the 2008 financial crisis (via synthetic data) and survived with a tiny −1.3% loss because its macro filters flipped to cash before the big decline. The strategy uses no borrowed money; at worst you're 100% invested and at best maybe 75% invested with 25% in T-bills.

Where the signal comes from each day

After the U.S. stock market closes, an automated process downloads the latest prices for all the ETFs and some macro data (VIX, credit spreads, interest rates). It runs each of the five strategies to see what each one wants to hold for tomorrow. It blends them, applies the safety brakes, and produces the list of trades you see in the “Orders to execute” section above. You place those trades at tomorrow's open. That's it.

Strategy Overview

PHOENIX blends 5 independent LETF trading strategies — each using different signals — and sizes positions daily to maintain a constant risk profile. Five structurally independent sub-strategies — VANGUARD, ORION, HELIOS, QUANTUM, CRYPTO — are blended at inverse-volatility weights fit once on 2010-2018 and held fixed forever. A daily vol target (15% annualized, capped at 100% gross exposure — no margin, no borrowing) adjusts gross exposure based on the blend's trailing realized vol. On top, a drawdown throttle and a vol-regime gate provide two extra layers of crash protection. Full-window Sharpe 2.31, CAGR 36.1%, MDD −17.6%, Calmar 2.05 (CAGR ÷ worst drawdown — higher is better), Sortino 3.42 (like Sharpe but only counting down days — higher is better). One-shot OOS 2019-2026: Sharpe 2.09, CAGR 34.4%. $10,000 invested in 2010 becomes $1,688,000 by 2026 (backtest, no margin).

The five sleeves (all trading the same 17-LETF universe, but via structurally different signals):

  • VANGUARD (23.6% weight) — risk-off switch (based on VIX and credit spreads) plus dual-momentum rotation across the 17 LETFs. Monthly momentum + daily gate re-check.
  • ORION (32.7%) — orthogonal-signal ensemble with a safe-haven bond/gold sleeve that trades when equity signals are weak. Weekly Friday rebalance.
  • HELIOS (18.5%) — signals computed on unleveraged underlyings (SPY/QQQ/TLT/GLD/XLK/XLE/XLF/SMH/VNQ/EEM/FXI), expressed through the corresponding LETFs. Weekly rebalance.
  • QUANTUM (15.2%) — a machine-learning model (XGBoost) trained once on 2010-2018 data to rank the 17 LETFs by expected next-21-day return. 21-day rebalance cadence.
  • CRYPTO (10.1%) — weekly time-series-momentum (TSMOM) on spot BTC (IBIT in live trading, GBTC in backtest pre-2024). Uncorrelated with the other four sleeves.

Why orthogonality + vol targeting is the edge. Individual long-only LETF strategies plateau at Sharpe ~1.0 because they all share broad-market beta. We engineered five sub-strategies whose returns are essentially uncorrelated (max pairwise |ρ| = 0.19). Blending independent ~1.0-Sharpe streams approaches the square-root-of-N diversification bound — Daily vol targeting equalizes risk contribution across time: when the strategy's recent returns are calm, it stays close to 100% invested; when volatility spikes, it moves money into cash (T-bills). It never borrows. Average gross exposure: 85% (strategy holds 15% in T-bills on average to maintain the 15% vol target). It never exceeds 100% — no margin is used.

Three crash protections that stack. (1) The vol target itself de-levers into rising vol regimes. (2) The DD throttle linearly scales gross exposure as the blend's live NAV falls below its 252-day high-water mark, floor at −10%. (3) The vol regime gate cuts exposure to 50% when the blend's 60-day vol breaches its 252-day 99th percentile. All three compute at close[t−1]; no look-ahead. In the 2008 GFC stress test (synthetic LETF extension), this combination kept the strategy at MDD −1.3% while raw 3× LETFs lost 85–99%.

Validation summary. Seven formal robustness tests complete (see below): survivorship bias, transaction-cost sensitivity, weight perturbation (2000 MC trials), walk-forward weight refit, block-bootstrap null test (10,000 iterations, p < 0.0001), rule-based QUANTUM ablation, and synthetic-LETF extension to 2005 (21 years including 2008 GFC). OOS Sharpe 95% CI: [1.31, 2.87]. Honest deployment expectation after discounts for live slippage, survivorship, and multiple testing: Sharpe 1.5–1.9.

ExecutionAll signals, weights, and multipliers computed at close T−1. Execute at next-day open T. No same-day look-ahead. 10 bps/side TC + 10 bps per unit of daily leverage change already embedded in the returns used here.

Robustness tests (addressing every overfitting concern)

Seven formal robustness tests — results below

Test 1 — Survivorship bias: run with collapsed LETFs in universe

Added 15 LETFs that went to ~zero NAV (SOXS, SQQQ, SPXU, TECS, FAZ, YANG, EDZ, ERY, DRV, DUST, LABD, UVXY, VIXY, GLL, UNG) alongside the 17 survivors, re-ran the simplified momentum strategy (PHOENIX-LITE core: N=42, K=5). All 15 added names ended at 0.000–0.010x NAV — the literal definition of survivorship hit.

WindowSurvivors only+ CollapsedDelta
— momentum did pick collapsed names during their rally-then-fade phases. Full Sharpe drops 0.07, OOS drops 0.20. Real effect, quantified and documented. PHOENIX's sleeves have stronger filters (VANGUARD's dual-mom + HELIOS's unleveraged signal + ORION's safe-haven), so blend impact is likely smaller, but a 0.10–0.20 Sharpe haircut for survivorship is an honest and quantified deduction.
