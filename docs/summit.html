The rules, in plain English

1 · Healthy market?

If the S&P 500 is above its 200-day average and most stocks are healthy, the model is in "leaders" mode. Otherwise it switches to "on-sale" mode.

2 · Leaders mode

Buy the two biggest companies that are also climbing the strongest (momentum over the last 3–12 months, leaning toward the largest names).

3 · On-sale mode

Keep buying — but switch to big, healthy companies trading 30–60% below their all-time high. Buy quality at a discount instead of chasing.

4 · Hold forever

Two names per buy, 50/50, at the next open. Never sell. No stop-losses, no panic exits, no cash-timing — all of those were tested and made it worse.

Why you can trust the numbers

  • No hindsight. The model only ever uses data available before each buy, and we verified this by re-running with the future deleted — zero difference.
  • No survivorship cheating. It uses the real S&P 500 membership at each point in history, including companies that later failed or were dropped.
  • Robust. Same result at 5 vs 40 bps trading cost, across every schedule start, and whether you buy biweekly, monthly, weekly or daily.
  • Honest about limits. A coin-flip random picker from the same list barely beats QQQ (8% of the time) — so the edge is the selection, not the list.
Caveats. Free price data is missing some companies that were delisted before ~2015, which can flatter older results a little — treat the long-run numbers as a touch optimistic. The portfolio is concentrated (today mostly NVDA and AAPL), so it can swing hard. Past performance doesn't guarantee future results. This is not investment advice.
